01 · Reading the maps

Three lenses on every crop

Bangladesh's 64 districts were processed through a shared pipeline: crop production and harvested-area figures from BBS, joined by DISTCODE to a district boundary shapefile, projected in Bangladesh Transverse Mercator. Each crop is read through three complementary parameters — no single one tells the whole story.

Y

Crop Yield

How productively land is being used — independent of farm scale. A district can cultivate widely yet yield poorly, or farm a small area with excellent output.

MT/ha = Production (MT) ÷ Harvested Area (ha)
A

Crop Area

The absolute extent under cultivation, in hectares. This is the production geography — which districts anchor national supply by sheer scale.

Sourced directly from BBS crop statistics
%

% of District Area

Crop area normalised against total district size — revealing genuine land specialisation rather than an artefact of a district simply being large.

% = (Crop Area ÷ Total District Area) × 100
Processing pipeline

From shapefile to symbology

  1. Yield calculationCrop production (MT) divided by harvested area (ha) for every district × crop combination.
  2. Geometry & areaDistrict area computed with the ArcGIS Pro Geometry Calculator, exported to DBF, linked in Excel to derive % crop area.
  3. Attribute joinProcessed Excel tables joined to the district shapefile on DISTCODE via Add Join.
  4. Graduated symbologyEqual-interval, five-class breaks applied per crop and parameter in ArcGIS Pro; charts built in Python (Matplotlib / Seaborn).

02 · Setting the baseline

Cropping Intensity — the land's calendar

Before reading any single crop, intensity sets the context: how many times a hectare is cultivated within one year. 100% is single cropping, 200% is double, and anything beyond reflects triple or mixed systems stacked onto the same land.

09 · Putting it together

The same map, six times over

Read side by side, the eighteen maps describe one recurring geography: an irrigated, alluvial northwest that leads on nearly every parameter, and a hydrologically constrained south and east that consistently trails — not from poor management, but from terrain, salinity, and the monsoon itself.

How concentrated is each crop?

Two staple rices, opposite logics

Boro & Aman anchor the calendar

Together Boro and Aman occupy land across nearly every district. Boro concentrates where groundwater irrigation exists — area and yield align in the same northwestern districts. Aman is the opposite: Bangladesh's most evenly spread crop, grown out of agro-ecological necessity wherever monsoon flooding or dry-season salinity leave no other option.

Aus, the marginal season

A rainfed crop in retreat

Aus persists mainly in the rainfed lowlands of Barisal, squeezed everywhere else by the Boro–Aman rotation. It is the most spatially restricted rice season nationally, with over half of all districts recording negligible area or land share.

Three crops, one belt

Wheat, Maize & Potato share an address

All three are climatically gated to the cool, dry winters and well-drained alluvial soils of Rajshahi and Rangpur. Maize and Potato share an almost identical concentration signature: 85–87% of districts negligible, with zero districts in the “High” percentage-area class — an abrupt jump from moderate to dominant, with no transition zone.

The recurring periphery

Sylhet, Chittagong & coastal Barisal

Across all six crops, the same districts recur at the low end — not from weak management but from haor flooding, hill terrain, and tidal salinity. These structural constraints, persistent across every map in this study, point toward the need for regionally differentiated agricultural strategy rather than a single national prescription.
